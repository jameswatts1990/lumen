Evidence notes
  1. BG1 Background warmth/brightness is most relevant for evening use and light sensitivity; evidence emphasizes sleep-timing and comfort outcomes more than comprehension. Target: mainly adults, with limited child-specific data.
  2. BG2 Amber tint can reduce short-wavelength light exposure. Reading benefit is not universal and often depends on preference, display brightness, and ambient light. Target: adults and adolescents; dyslexia-specific effects unclear.
  3. D1 Line length and zoom have moderate support for improving readability and reducing eye movement strain, but best ranges differ by acuity and task. Target: adults and older students; applies to dyslexia and non-dyslexia readers with individual tuning.
  4. T1 Typeface changes show mixed objective effects; subjective ease and sustained use often improve when users choose their own font. Target: broad population, including dyslexia and non-dyslexia readers.
  5. T2 Extra spacing can support decoding accuracy for some dyslexic readers and developing readers, but can disrupt word-shape recognition for others. Target: children with decoding challenges and some adults with dyslexia.
  6. F1 Reading rulers and pointer guides can improve line tracking, especially for readers who lose place often. Benefit is variable and may drop with distraction sensitivity. Target: children learning fluency and adults with attentional load.
  7. F2 Local contrast/shading can focus attention but may raise visual clutter if too strong. Use lighter settings first. Target: mixed populations; individualized comfort threshold.
  8. F3 Chunked presentation can aid stepwise reading and metering, particularly for struggling readers, yet may constrain global comprehension for fluent readers. Target: children and adults with decoding needs.
  9. F4 Peripheral dimming can reduce distraction for some users, while others rely on peripheral context cues. Target: mixed; preference-dependent across dyslexia and non-dyslexia readers.
  10. A1 Auto pacing can lower regressions and improve rhythm for practice sessions, but comprehension can decline if speed is mismatched. Target: adults and adolescents; use with frequent comprehension checks.
