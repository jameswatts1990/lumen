Evidence Notes
Research notes behind the evidence labels used across controls.
  1. BG1 Background warmth/brightness is most relevant for evening use and light sensitivity; evidence emphasizes sleep-timing and comfort outcomes more than comprehension. Target: mainly adults, with limited child-specific data. (Cajochen et al., 2011; Chang et al., 2015; Hale & Guan, 2015)
  2. BG2 Amber tint can reduce short-wavelength light exposure. Reading benefit is not universal and often depends on preference, display brightness, and ambient light. Target: adults and adolescents; dyslexia-specific effects unclear. (Burkhart & Phelps, 2009; Ostrin et al., 2017; Ritchie et al., 2011)
  3. D1 Line length and zoom have moderate support for improving readability and reducing eye movement strain, but best ranges differ by acuity and task. Target: adults and older students; applies to dyslexia and non-dyslexia readers with individual tuning. (Dyson & Kipping, 1998; Bernard et al., 2002; Rayner, 1998)
  4. T1 Typeface changes show mixed objective effects; subjective ease and sustained use often improve when users choose their own font. Target: broad population, including dyslexia and non-dyslexia readers. (Rello & Baeza-Yates, 2013; Kuster et al., 2018; Wery & Diliberto, 2017)
  5. T2 Extra spacing can support decoding accuracy for some dyslexic readers and developing readers, but can disrupt word-shape recognition for others. Target: children with decoding challenges and some adults with dyslexia. (Zorzi et al., 2012; Perea et al., 2012; van den Boer & Hakvoort, 2015)
  6. F1 Reading rulers and pointer guides can improve line tracking, especially for readers who lose place often. Benefit is variable and may drop with distraction sensitivity. Target: children learning fluency and adults with attentional load. (Ritchey & Goeke, 2006; Kim et al., 2014; Schneps et al., 2013)
  7. F2 Local contrast/shading can focus attention but may raise visual clutter if too strong. Use lighter settings first. Target: mixed populations; individualized comfort threshold. (Buchner et al., 2009; Borkin et al., 2016; Bernard et al., 2002)
  8. F3 Chunked presentation can aid stepwise reading and metering, particularly for struggling readers, yet may constrain global comprehension for fluent readers. Target: children and adults with decoding needs. (Korat et al., 2013; Wise et al., 2010; Sherman et al., 2013)
  9. F4 Peripheral dimming can reduce distraction for some users, while others rely on peripheral context cues. Target: mixed; preference-dependent across dyslexia and non-dyslexia readers. (Rayner, 1998; Schneps et al., 2013; Godwin et al., 2015)
  10. A1 Auto pacing can lower regressions and improve rhythm for practice sessions, but comprehension can decline if speed is mismatched. Target: adults and adolescents; use with frequent comprehension checks. (Juola et al., 1982; Beymer et al., 2008; Godwin et al., 2015)
  11. A2 Rapid serial visual presentation and speed-reading drills can increase throughput, but comprehension and delayed recall often decline at higher rates; brief phrase-level pauses can partially offset this for some readers. Target: mainly adults; use as optional training, not default sustained reading mode. (Potter, 1984; Rayner et al., 2016; Schotter et al., 2014)
